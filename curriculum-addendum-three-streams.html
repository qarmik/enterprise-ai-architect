Three Streams and Execution Discipline

An addendum to the Enterprise AI Systems Architect curriculum. Provenance: strategic observation by ChatGPT (Commander V). Reviewed, adjudicated, and structured by Claude. Accepted elements only.

Adjudication Verdict
Accepted: The three-stream mental model and the psychological warning about overdesign before implementation.

Rejected: Hackathons as a structural addition. Parallel "small builds" track as a separate obligation.

Reason: The curriculum already contains all three streams. This addendum names them explicitly so they are visible — it does not add new activities or fragment the constrained 7–10 hour weekly budget.
Addendum · Section A
The Three Streams
The curriculum has always contained three simultaneous streams of work. This section names them explicitly so you can see what you are doing and why each stream is necessary.
§ A.1
A.1 — What the Three Streams Are
Any serious technical transformation has three things running simultaneously. When one stream collapses, the other two become unstable. The curriculum was designed with all three present — this section makes that visible.
Stream 1
Curriculum
Deep capability accumulation. Structured, sequenced, phase-gated. Moves you from foundational fluency to enterprise architecture depth over 42 months.
How it appears in this curriculum
The six phases. The ADR collection. The operational mathematics thread. The governance thread. The benchmark reports. The postmortems.
Stream 2
Artifacts
Execution realism. Built things that run. Exposes false understanding that theory cannot detect. Forces precision about what "working" means.
How it appears in this curriculum
The 14 artifacts. The test files. The CI pipeline. The benchmark reports with real numbers. The brownfield rescue exercise. The capstone system.
Stream 3
Public Writing
Intellectual visibility. Forces clarity of thought. Builds external credibility. Makes growth legible to others and to yourself.
How it appears in this curriculum
The deload week post after each phase. The community post every Sunday from Phase 2. The ADR collection as public documentation. The GitHub repository itself.
Why All Three Are Already Present
The curriculum was not missing any of these streams. The addendum names them because naming them helps you recognise when one is being neglected. If a week passes with no artifact progress, Stream 2 is starving. If three months pass with no public post, Stream 3 is starving. The weekly review question "did you build anything this week?" is Stream 2's health check. The Sunday community post ritual is Stream 3's health check.
§ A.2
A.2 — What Happens When a Stream Collapses
Each failure mode is a real trap. The curriculum's structure guards against all three — but the guard only works if you understand what you are being guarded against.
Three Failure Modes
Stream 1 collapses (no curriculum): Shallow hype-builder culture. Framework-chasing. Demo-capable, systems-ignorant. Can ship fast, cannot explain why anything works.
Stream 2 collapses (no artifacts): Intellectually interesting, cannot ship. Deep conceptual understanding that has never been stress-tested by implementation. Avoidance through sophistication. The specific risk for someone with strong systems cognition and a full-time demanding job.
Stream 3 collapses (no writing): Invisible growth. 42 months of accumulated capability that nobody outside your Codespace can see. The portfolio exists but cannot speak for itself. Technical communities remain unaware you exist.
Your Specific Risk — Stream 2
The observation from ChatGPT is accurate and worth preserving: your temperament naturally drifts toward systems philosophy, architecture frameworks, and conceptual synthesis. These are genuine strengths. Without shipping pressure they can become avoidance through sophistication — endless refinement of the curriculum, endless architectural thinking, insufficient contact with the implementation reality that exposes false understanding. The done definition protocol and the artifact-first principle exist specifically to counteract this. They are not bureaucratic rituals. They are the mechanism that keeps Stream 2 alive.

Addendum · Section B
Execution Discipline
The psychological warning from the external review, preserved in full because it is accurate and relevant throughout all 42 months.
§ B.1
B.1 — The Overdesign Warning
The institutional systems cognition that makes you effective in Phase 4 governance work is a liability in Phase 0-2 prototyping. The SBI instinct toward procedural completeness, operational robustness, and structural thoroughness is earned and real. Applied prematurely to early artifact building, it produces over-engineered prototypes, infinite planning loops, and the particular failure mode of avoidance through sophistication.
The Rule for Early Phases
Early systems should be ugly but alive. A working ugly system teaches more than a beautifully designed system that does not yet exist. You can refine later. You cannot learn from something that has not run. The done definition protocol enforces this — it defines done as a specific running output, not as a specific quality level.
§ B.1.1
Recognising Overdesign in Real Time
You are overdesigning when any of these are true during a session:
Overdesign Signals
You are refining the architecture of something that has not yet run once
You are reading about the right way to structure something instead of structuring it badly and running it
You are adding error handling to code that has not yet produced a correct output on the happy path
You are writing documentation for a system whose behaviour you do not yet understand from running it
The session timer is at 60 minutes and nothing has been committed
When you notice any of these: stop. Write the ugliest version that could possibly run. Run it. Commit it. Refine in the next session.
§ B.2
B.2 — What Was Rejected and Why
The external review proposed hackathons and a parallel small-builds track as structural additions. These were reviewed and rejected. The reasoning is recorded here so the decision is not revisited without new information.
Rejected — Hackathons
A single hackathon weekend consumes an entire week's study budget and produces an artifact that does not fit the curriculum's phase structure. The compressed execution environment that hackathons provide is already present in the curriculum: the 90-minute timer on benchmark report sections, the brownfield rescue exercise, the commit-on-timer-end rule. Adding hackathons does not add compressed execution — it adds calendar disruption and a parallel artifact that competes with the primary build track.
Rejected — Parallel Small-Builds Track
The curriculum artifacts are the small public builds. They are deployed, public, and operational. Adding a separate track of "small builds" alongside the curriculum artifacts fragments the 7–10 hour weekly budget and produces two shallow things instead of one deep thing. The risk is not that the curriculum lacks execution realism — it is that execution realism requires all available time to be directed at the primary artifact, not divided across multiple parallel projects.
When This Decision Should Be Revisited
If by Phase 3 the weekly budget has consistently exceeded 10 hours and all curriculum artifacts are on track, a small parallel build becomes viable. The trigger is surplus capacity, not enthusiasm. The condition for revisiting this decision is: Phase 3 artifacts are ahead of schedule AND weekly hours are averaging above 10. If both conditions are not true, the decision stands.
§ C
C — The Single Sentence This Addendum Reduces To
The curriculum already contains all three streams — curriculum depth, artifact execution, and public writing. Your job is to keep all three alive simultaneously, resist the specific failure mode of avoidance through sophistication, and trust that ugly working things teach more than beautiful unbuilt designs.
